# Market-led AI product catalogue

Research date: 2026-07-15

## Executive conclusion

Armalo should keep a broad catalogue because the team can build across many
workflows, but it should not pretend that build capability equals demand. The
commercial front door is a ranked set of ten hypotheses selected from two kinds
of evidence:

1. independent market research showing where companies and consumers are
   already adopting or spending on AI; and
2. first-party operator pages showing how high-ticket AI offers are packaged,
   positioned, delivered, and expanded.

The independent sources support sales and marketing, service operations,
software engineering, knowledge work, horizontal copilots, vertical AI, and
consumer assistants as active categories. The operator sources support a
repeatable selling pattern: choose a narrow buyer and outcome, package existing
capabilities into a system, sell an initial implementation, then retain the
customer for access, optimization, support, or a larger operating layer.

Willingness to pay for these Armalo products remains unproven.

## What the named operators actually sell

### Jordan Lee and AI Acquisition

AI Acquisition's public material sells an AI-services business model more than
one end-user application. Its stated playbook is to choose an accessible niche,
tie an AI-enabled solution to a business outcome, implement modular existing
tools, and move from one-off projects toward recurring optimization and
support. Its platform positioning adds an agency operating layer for finding
clients and managing delivery.

What Armalo should learn:

- lead with the job and buyer, not the model;
- make the first implementation narrow and fast;
- keep a reusable fulfillment core behind niche-specific packaging; and
- sell ongoing evidence, tuning, and support only after the workflow works.

What Armalo should not copy:

- broad earnings implications;
- testimonials as proof of a typical result;
- a generic promise that any buyer can run an AI agency; or
- an arbitrage posture that treats technical and operational quality as
  interchangeable commodities.

The AI Acquisition page is first-party marketing. Its pricing, margin, and
customer-result statements describe its positioning; they are not independent
evidence that Armalo buyers will pay the same amount or achieve similar results.

### Alex Hormozi and Acquisition.com

Acquisition.com currently exposes ACQ AI, a business advisor grounded in Alex
Hormozi's books, frameworks, and consulting material. The product pattern is
more important than the personality: proprietary knowledge becomes an
interactive, source-bounded advisor that extends a broader media, book,
workshop, and investment funnel.

What Armalo should learn:

- a trusted corpus is a product asset;
- the assistant should answer within a recognizable decision framework;
- free or accessible knowledge can qualify demand for deeper services; and
- the human expert, source library, and commercial offer should reinforce one
  another.

Armalo should not market an assistant as someone's brain, impersonate an
expert, or infer private opinions. The catalogue therefore uses `Expert
Knowledge Assistant`, with source rights, citations, identity language,
correction workflows, and escalation as part of the product contract.

### Iman Gadzhi and Monetise

Monetise's terms identify a bundle that includes AI software products such as
GhostwriterOS, Synthesise AI, and AskIman alongside education and community
access. Public promotional material positions AI as a way to research, create,
package, market, and deliver digital products.

The defensible pattern is a joined product system:

- training explains the method;
- software performs repeatable parts of the work;
- coaching handles ambiguity and accountability;
- community provides examples, momentum, and distribution; and
- the resulting product creates another revenue surface.

Armalo's version is `AI Digital Product Studio`. It begins with owned expertise
and audience evidence, runs a proof-of-value pilot, and treats trust, refunds,
quality, rights, and outcome evidence as first-class constraints. Promotional
earnings claims do not count as proof that a product category has durable buyer
demand.

### Serge Gatari, ClientAcquisition.io, and Cook AI

ClientAcquisition.io publicly positions a build-and-release model for AI growth
infrastructure, including systems spanning lead generation, nurturing, closing,
and AI dialing. Cook AI's current workshop material goes further: encode the
operator's playbook and judgment into an AI-native delivery system so the
service business can operate with software-like leverage.

The useful product and deal patterns are:

- diagnose a valuable growth bottleneck;
- sell a bounded build-and-release engagement;
- encode the operating playbook, not just isolated prompts;
- make the system reusable across a vertical;
- charge for implementation before claiming a platform; and
- expand into recurring operations after the first workflow is proven.

Armalo maps this pattern to `Hermes Revenue Agents`, `AI Dialer`, `Hermes AI
CRM`, and the broader `Autonomous Business` operating layer. The public
catalogue keeps each module separately buyable so a customer does not need to
buy the whole vision before one result exists.

### Alex Becker and HYROS

The platform is HYROS, not Hyrox. HYROX is a fitness-racing brand; HYROS is the
advertising attribution and optimization platform relevant to this research.

HYROS combines customer-journey and transaction attribution with ad-platform
optimization, forecasting, and an AIR remarketing agent. The strategic lesson
is that AI performance improves when the product owns a useful evidence loop:
source, journey, conversion, customer value, action, and outcome.

Armalo's corresponding product is `AI Attribution & Remarketing`. It does not
claim perfect attribution or copy HYROS. It focuses on reconciling evidence,
explaining uncertainty, recommending spend decisions, and using approved
behavior for accountable follow-up.

Armalo has no affiliation with AI Acquisition, Acquisition.com, Monetise,
ClientAcquisition.io, Cook AI, HYROS, their founders, or their products. Their
names appear only as public market and packaging references.

## Independent market evidence

McKinsey's 2025 state-of-AI research reports that organizations most often use
generative AI in marketing and sales, product and service development, service
operations, software engineering, and IT. It also cautions that broad adoption
has not automatically produced material enterprise-level earnings impact. That
supports narrow outcome contracts and measured pilots rather than catalogue
hype.

Menlo Ventures' 2025 enterprise report describes substantial application-layer
spending in horizontal copilots, agent platforms, personal productivity, and
vertical AI. Its prior departmental breakdown also places meaningful budgets in
IT, product and engineering, support, sales, marketing, finance, design, and
legal. This supports a portfolio that spans both horizontal knowledge systems
and vertical workflow products.

Menlo's consumer research and a16z's shopping analysis describe growing use of
AI for learning, research, everyday assistance, and more personalized commerce.
These sources support investigating consumer assistants, but they do not prove
that a standalone AI stylist will convert. `AI Stylist` remains the tenth and
most exploratory commercial priority.

## The build-to-sell shortlist

### 1. Hermes Revenue Agents

Buyer: high-ticket teams, agencies, and service businesses.

Purchased job: create more qualified sales conversations while preserving the
evidence and human handoff behind each opportunity.

Initial offer: one pipeline segment, one ideal-customer profile, one approved
outreach path, and one measurable conversion boundary.

Expansion: lead generation, qualification, setting, calling, proposal support,
CRM state, and full-cycle coordination.

### 2. AI Customer Service Desk

Buyer: support and operations teams with repetitive demand across channels.

Purchased job: reduce routine workload and response delay without hiding
exceptions or customer risk.

Initial offer: one channel and the top approved intents, with human escalation.

Expansion: the parent service layer shares policy, knowledge, identity,
routing, and reporting across the existing email, SMS, voice, and WhatsApp
modules.

### 3. AI Dialer

Buyer: local services, high-ticket teams, and call-based funnels.

Purchased job: respond, qualify, schedule, and transfer quickly inside a
versioned script and consent policy.

Initial offer: one inbound or explicitly consented follow-up workflow.

Expansion: dispositions, objection learning, scheduling, CRM state, and human
closer handoff.

### 4. Hermes AI CRM

Buyer: founders, agencies, and sales teams whose automation has fragmented
pipeline truth.

Purchased job: keep identity, opportunity state, agent proposals, approvals,
deliveries, replies, and revenue evidence in one accountable record.

Initial offer: a canonical lead and activity record for one funnel.

Expansion: revenue agents, service history, attribution, forecasting, and
operator reporting.

### 5. Internal Knowledge Assistant

Buyer: professional-services, support, and operating teams.

Purchased job: answer recurring questions from approved company knowledge with
citations, permissions, and visible freshness.

Initial offer: one team, one source collection, and a representative evaluation
set.

Expansion: more teams, workflow actions, correction ownership, and analytics on
knowledge gaps.

### 6. AI Forward Deployed Engineer

Buyer: software companies and operating businesses with one valuable workflow
stuck between a demo and production.

Purchased job: supply an accountable human-and-agent delivery team that can
diagnose, integrate, evaluate, pilot, and transfer the workflow.

Initial offer: a fixed diagnostic followed by one bounded production pilot.

Expansion: recurring embedded capacity, reusable integration patterns, and a
managed operating lane.

### 7. AI Attribution & Remarketing

Buyer: paid-growth teams, ecommerce brands, SaaS companies, and agencies.

Purchased job: understand which journeys create valuable customers and use
approved behavior to improve targeting and follow-up.

Initial offer: reconcile one funnel's source, conversion, and customer-value
evidence before adding automated action.

Expansion: multi-channel attribution, forecasting, audience feedback, and
consent-aware remarketing.

### 8. AI Digital Product Studio

Buyer: creators, educators, experts, and agencies.

Purchased job: turn owned expertise and real audience problems into a coherent
course, guide, community, coaching offer, or hybrid product.

Initial offer: a short research and proof-of-value sprint with a small cohort.

Expansion: product library, launch assets, delivery, community, licensing, and
revenue-share partnerships.

### 9. Expert Knowledge Assistant

Buyer: experts, educators, membership businesses, and advisory firms.

Purchased job: make a trusted body of work interactive without unsupported
impersonation or advice.

Initial offer: one approved corpus, citations, identity boundaries, and a
representative question evaluation.

Expansion: subscriptions, course inclusion, enterprise licensing, multilingual
access, and qualified human escalation.

### 10. AI Stylist

Buyer: consumers, personal stylists, retailers, and fashion brands.

Purchased job: turn wardrobe, occasion, preference, taste, and inventory
context into fewer, better, explainable choices.

Initial offer: wardrobe and outfit planning without checkout authority.

Expansion: retailer inventory, white-label experiences, affiliate commerce,
returns learning, and human-stylist collaboration.

## One catalogue, multiple deal shapes

Every product can be sold through the same six-step commercial ladder:

1. **Diagnostic.** Observe the workflow, baseline, buyer, authority, data,
   exceptions, and economic value.
2. **Proof-of-value pilot.** Handle a narrow slice with representative cases
   and visible human oversight.
3. **Implementation.** Connect approved systems, permissions, logging,
   evaluation, escalation, and rollback.
4. **Recurring optimization.** Charge for monitored access, tuning, support,
   evidence review, and workflow improvement.
5. **Licensed or hosted product.** Standardize only after repeated delivery
   shows the common core.
6. **Agency or partner package.** Let another operator own distribution or
   client context while Armalo protects product, evidence, and fulfillment
   boundaries.

The default sales sequence is diagnostic fee, scoped pilot, implementation,
then recurring access or optimization. Commission or revenue share is reserved
for cases where attribution, authority, delivery cost, and payout terms can be
measured without argument.

## What Armalo can replicate—and what it should not

Replicate:

- outcome-led positioning;
- narrow vertical entry points;
- build-and-release implementation;
- proprietary knowledge products;
- software plus service plus education packaging;
- recurring optimization and support;
- modular products that combine into an operating suite;
- evidence loops that improve delivery over time; and
- partner, agency, licensing, and revenue-share options when measurement is
  strong.

Do not replicate:

- names, trademarks, visual identity, proprietary content, prompts, or code;
- unsupported income, margin, lift, or customer-result claims;
- artificial scarcity or urgency as a substitute for value;
- testimonial selection presented as a typical outcome;
- autonomous authority before consent, policy, or proof; or
- ten simultaneous builds before one buyer in each lane validates the problem.

The correct interpretation of “there is nothing we cannot build” is that the
catalogue can remain expansive. The commercial system must remain selective:
validate the first buyer, baseline, authority, delivery path, and economic
result before moving a product from planned inventory to a stronger claim.

## Next validation loop

For each priority, Armalo should collect five pieces of evidence before changing
its catalogue proof status:

1. a named buyer and workflow owner;
2. current volume, time, cost, quality, conversion, or backlog baseline;
3. a bounded pilot with representative cases and stop conditions;
4. delivery evidence showing what the system actually did; and
5. an observed business result plus a customer-safe account of limitations.

The shortlist should be reviewed monthly. A product moves up when buyer calls,
paid diagnostics, pilots, retention, or business-verified proof improve. It
moves down when evidence shows weak urgency, integration cost, compliance risk,
poor adoption, or inferior economics. Breadth belongs in the catalogue;
commercial focus belongs in the ranked shortlist.
